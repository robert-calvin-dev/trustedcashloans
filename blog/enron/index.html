What Exactly Happened at Enron?

Published by Trusted Cash Loans • May 29, 2025

Enron went from Wall Street darling to cautionary tale almost overnight. At its peak, it was America’s seventh-largest company. Within months, it was bankrupt, and its name became synonymous with corporate fraud. Here’s how it all unraveled.

The Rise

Founded in 1985, Enron transformed from a regional energy provider into a global energy trader. Its complex trading business and bold marketing made it a media favorite. But behind the buzz, the numbers didn’t add up.

Accounting Gymnastics

Enron used "mark-to-market" accounting, booking profits based on future projections—not actual income. They also hid debt in off-balance-sheet entities called Special Purpose Entities (SPEs). It looked like growth. It was debt in disguise.

The Players

CEO Jeff Skilling and Chairman Ken Lay were the faces of Enron. CFO Andrew Fastow built the shady financial structures. Auditing firm Arthur Andersen turned a blind eye—until it was too late.

The Collapse

In late 2001, analysts started asking questions. Confidence evaporated. Stock prices plummeted. Enron filed for bankruptcy in December 2001. Thousands lost jobs, pensions, and life savings.

The Fallout

Executives went to prison. Arthur Andersen shut down. And the Sarbanes-Oxley Act was born—new federal regulations to prevent future frauds of this scale.

The Enron lesson? If the numbers seem too good to be true, they probably are.
