10 Biggest Get Rich Quick Scheme Flops of the 90’s

Published by Trusted Cash Loans • May 29, 2025

The 90s were neon bright, full of dial-up tones—and ripe for scams. The “get rich quick” mentality exploded in the pre-dot-com boom, with wannabe moguls chasing shortcuts to wealth. Here's a cautionary countdown of ten unforgettable failures.

1. The Beanie Baby Bubble

People mortgaged homes to buy plush toys. Ty Inc. discontinued characters to create artificial scarcity—and then flooded the market. Today? Most are worth less than a Happy Meal toy.

2. Excel Communications

Excel sold long-distance service through a multi-level marketing (MLM) model. It was the golden child of telecom MLMs—until cell phones destroyed long-distance bills, and the company collapsed.

3. Sports Card Speculation

Collectors hoarded rookie cards expecting fortune. Overproduction and lack of rarity tanked the market. The result? Shoe boxes full of worthless cardboard.

4. 1-900 Phone Scams

Call a psychic! Get rich tips! Rack up a $300 phone bill! The FTC cracked down in the late 90s, but by then, millions had already been drained one minute at a time.

5. The Clinton Million Fax Club

This MLM promised payouts for sending fax-based offers. Like early spam. It made money—for the top 1%. The rest just paid for fax paper.

6. Psychic Hotline Investments

Dionne Warwick fronted the movement, but no one saw the collapse coming. Turns out, vague advice isn’t a solid investment strategy.

7. Amway Recruiting Blitz

While still active today, Amway's 90s heyday saw ultra-aggressive recruiting tactics. Many walked away with debt—not profits.

8. Chain Letter Gifting Circles

“Send $100 to the person at the top.” This pyramid-style “gifting” scheme preyed on trust and small-town hope. Most participants got nothing back.

9. Magazine Subscription Hustles

Earn cash reselling overpriced magazine subscriptions door-to-door! Except you didn’t. These were thinly veiled commission scams with little to no payout.

10. Internet Gold Rush Scams

With dot-com stocks booming, grifters sold “internet startup kits” or “domain flipping blueprints” with fake promises. When the bubble burst in 2000, so did the dreams.

If it sounds too good to be true—it’s a 90s scam waiting to happen.
